Sports Retail — User Behavior, Segmentation & Propensity Model

  • Research (IPYNB)
  • Report (PDF)
  • Presentation (PDF)

Client prepares a marketing campaign in city 1188 (country 32). The goal: assess user behavior, cluster customers, build a purchase propensity model, and deliver recommendations for personalized marketing.

Data

Integrated tables covering user profiles (sex, age, education, city/country), customer coefficients, detailed purchases (product, color, gender tag, discount flag/size, price, date), and history of a prior email A/B test. Scope: 104,534 unique customers and 786,260 purchases.

Behavior Highlights

  • Typical buyer ≈ 36 y.o.; ~6 purchases; total spend ≈ 34k units.
  • Most have secondary education; apparel & footwear dominate the basket.
  • Older users show stronger interest in discounts.

A/B Test (Email)

  • Conversion rate: control = 1.00, test = 1.00 (no change).
  • Avg. number of purchases: 11.02 → 12.80 in test (p = 0.0057).
  • Avg. spend and discount share: no significant difference.
  • Conclusion: email increases purchase frequency, not average check.

Clustering

Selected k = 3 clusters (silhouette peak and elbow concur). Profiles: Cluster 0 — middle-aged, low activity, secondary education; Cluster 1 — younger, low activity, higher education; Cluster 2 — anomalously active, very high spend.

Propensity-to-Purchase Model

Trained on the A/B sample (target: test vs control). Algorithm: RandomForestClassifier. Features: age, sex, spend, discounts, cluster, education. Results: F1 = 0.71 (class 1), Recall = 0.80, AUC = 0.51. Interpretation: high recall suitable for broad outreach; weak separation overall.

Discount Sensitivity

High-response customers are less discount-driven than low/medium groups; clusters differ in discount usage distributions.

High-Response Portrait

  • Mostly 40+; activity ~6–7 purchases; spend ≈ 33–35k.
  • Often secondary education; prefer apparel, footwear, equipment.
  • Discount is not a key motivator.

Recommendations

  • Cluster 0 (mass): content-driven comms (curated picks, guides, reviews); emphasize value & service; avoid blanket deep discounts.
  • Cluster 1 (younger, digital): visual/gamified mechanics, mobile pushes, convenience (fast selection, one-click).
  • Cluster 2 (VIP-like): exclusivity, premium loyalty, personal managers; status & service over mass promos.
  • General: behavior-based segmentation outperforms pure demographics; test segment-specific creatives and discount levels.

Limitations

  • Small training sample from A/B; no explicit negative outcomes; potential seasonality/geo bias.
